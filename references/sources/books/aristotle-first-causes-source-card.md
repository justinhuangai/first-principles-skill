**Metaphysics**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)

By Aristotle [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)

Written 350 B.C.E

Translated by W. D. Ross

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)**Part 1**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)

"ALL men by nature desire to know. An indication of this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)is the delight we take in our senses; for even apart from their usefulness [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)they are loved for themselves; and above all others the sense of sight. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)For not only with a view to action, but even when we are not going to do [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)anything, we prefer seeing (one might say) to everything else. The reason [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)is that this, most of all the senses, makes us know and brings to light [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)many differences between things.

"By nature animals are born with [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the faculty of sensation, and from sensation memory is produced in some [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of them, though not in others. And therefore the former are more intelligent [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and apt at learning than those which cannot remember; those which are incapable [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of hearing sounds are intelligent though they cannot be taught, e.g. the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)bee, and any other race of animals that may be like it; and those which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)besides memory have this sense of hearing can be taught.

"The animals [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)other than man live by appearances and memories, and have but little of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)connected experience; but the human race lives also by art and reasonings. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)Now from memory experience is produced in men; for the several memories [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of the same thing produce finally the capacity for a single experience. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)And experience seems pretty much like science and art, but really science [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and art come to men through experience; for 'experience made art', as Polus [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)says, 'but inexperience luck.' Now art arises when from many notions gained [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)by experience one universal judgement about a class of objects is produced. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)For to have a judgement that when Callias was ill of this disease this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)did him good, and similarly in the case of Socrates and in many individual [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)cases, is a matter of experience; but to judge that it has done good to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)all persons of a certain constitution, marked off in one class, when they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)were ill of this disease, e.g. to phlegmatic or bilious people when burning [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)with fevers-this is a matter of art.

"With a view to action experience [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)seems in no respect inferior to art, and men of experience succeed even [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)better than those who have theory without experience. (The reason is that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)experience is knowledge of individuals, art of universals, and actions [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and productions are all concerned with the individual; for the physician [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)does not cure man, except in an incidental way, but Callias or Socrates [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)or some other called by some such individual name, who happens to be a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)man. If, then, a man has the theory without the experience, and recognizes [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the universal but does not know the individual included in this, he will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)often fail to cure; for it is the individual that is to be cured.) But [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)yet we think that knowledge and understanding belong to art rather than [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to experience, and we suppose artists to be wiser than men of experience [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)(which implies that Wisdom depends in all cases rather on knowledge); and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)this because the former know the cause, but the latter do not. For men [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of experience know that the thing is so, but do not know why, while the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)others know the 'why' and the cause. Hence we think also that the masterworkers [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)in each craft are more honourable and know in a truer sense and are wiser [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)than the manual workers, because they know the causes of the things that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)are done (we think the manual workers are like certain lifeless things [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)which act indeed, but act without knowing what they do, as fire burns,-but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)while the lifeless things perform each of their functions by a natural [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)tendency, the labourers perform them through habit); thus we view them [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)as being wiser not in virtue of being able to act, but of having the theory [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)for themselves and knowing the causes. And in general it is a sign of the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)man who knows and of the man who does not know, that the former can teach, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and therefore we think art more truly knowledge than experience is; for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)artists can teach, and men of mere experience cannot.

"Again, we [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)do not regard any of the senses as Wisdom; yet surely these give the most [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)authoritative knowledge of particulars. But they do not tell us the 'why' [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of anything-e.g. why fire is hot; they only say that it is hot.

"At [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)first he who invented any art whatever that went beyond the common perceptions [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of man was naturally admired by men, not only because there was something [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)useful in the inventions, but because he was thought wise and superior [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to the rest. But as more arts were invented, and some were directed to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the necessities of life, others to recreation, the inventors of the latter [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)were naturally always regarded as wiser than the inventors of the former, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)because their branches of knowledge did not aim at utility. Hence when [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)all such inventions were already established, the sciences which do not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)aim at giving pleasure or at the necessities of life were discovered, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)first in the places where men first began to have leisure. This is why [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the mathematical arts were founded in Egypt; for there the priestly caste [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)was allowed to be at leisure.

"We have said in the Ethics what [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the difference is between art and science and the other kindred faculties; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)but the point of our present discussion is this, that all men suppose what [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)is called Wisdom to deal with the first causes and the principles of things; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)so that, as has been said before, the man of experience is thought to be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)wiser than the possessors of any sense-perception whatever, the artist [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)wiser than the men of experience, the masterworker than the mechanic, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the theoretical kinds of knowledge to be more of the nature of Wisdom than [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the productive. Clearly then Wisdom is knowledge about certain principles [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and causes.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)**Part 2**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)"

"Since we are seeking this knowledge, we must inquire of what kind [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)are the causes and the principles, the knowledge of which is Wisdom. If [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)one were to take the notions we have about the wise man, this might perhaps [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)make the answer more evident. We suppose first, then, that the wise man [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)knows all things, as far as possible, although he has not knowledge of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)each of them in detail; secondly, that he who can learn things that are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)difficult, and not easy for man to know, is wise (sense-perception is common [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to all, and therefore easy and no mark of Wisdom); again, that he who is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)more exact and more capable of teaching the causes is wiser, in every branch [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of knowledge; and that of the sciences, also, that which is desirable on [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)its own account and for the sake of knowing it is more of the nature of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)Wisdom than that which is desirable on account of its results, and the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)superior science is more of the nature of Wisdom than the ancillary; for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the wise man must not be ordered but must order, and he must not obey another, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)but the less wise must obey him.

"Such and so many are the notions, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)then, which we have about Wisdom and the wise. Now of these characteristics [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)that of knowing all things must belong to him who has in the highest degree [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)universal knowledge; for he knows in a sense all the instances that fall [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)under the universal. And these things, the most universal, are on the whole [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the hardest for men to know; for they are farthest from the senses. And [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the most exact of the sciences are those which deal most with first principles; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)for those which involve fewer principles are more exact than those which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)involve additional principles, e.g. arithmetic than geometry. But the science [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)which investigates causes is also instructive, in a higher degree, for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the people who instruct us are those who tell the causes of each thing. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)And understanding and knowledge pursued for their own sake are found most [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)in the knowledge of that which is most knowable (for he who chooses to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)know for the sake of knowing will choose most readily that which is most [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)truly knowledge, and such is the knowledge of that which is most knowable); [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and the first principles and the causes are most knowable; for by reason [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of these, and from these, all other things come to be known, and not these [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)by means of the things subordinate to them. And the science which knows [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to what end each thing must be done is the most authoritative of the sciences, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and more authoritative than any ancillary science; and this end is the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)good of that thing, and in general the supreme good in the whole of nature. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)Judged by all the tests we have mentioned, then, the name in question falls [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to the same science; this must be a science that investigates the first [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)principles and causes; for the good, i.e. the end, is one of the causes. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)

"That it is not a science of production is clear even from the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)history of the earliest philosophers. For it is owing to their wonder that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)men both now begin and at first began to philosophize; they wondered originally [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)at the obvious difficulties, then advanced little by little and stated [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)difficulties about the greater matters, e.g. about the phenomena of the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)moon and those of the sun and of the stars, and about the genesis of the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)universe. And a man who is puzzled and wonders thinks himself ignorant [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)(whence even the lover of myth is in a sense a lover of Wisdom, for the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)myth is composed of wonders); therefore since they philosophized order [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to escape from ignorance, evidently they were pursuing science in order [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to know, and not for any utilitarian end. And this is confirmed by the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)facts; for it was when almost all the necessities of life and the things [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)that make for comfort and recreation had been secured, that such knowledge [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)began to be sought. Evidently then we do not seek it for the sake of any [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)other advantage; but as the man is free, we say, who exists for his own [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)sake and not for another's, so we pursue this as the only free science, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)for it alone exists for its own sake.

"Hence also the possession [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of it might be justly regarded as beyond human power; for in many ways [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)human nature is in bondage, so that according to Simonides 'God alone can [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)have this privilege', and it is unfitting that man should not be content [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to seek the knowledge that is suited to him. If, then, there is something [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)in what the poets say, and jealousy is natural to the divine power, it [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)would probably occur in this case above all, and all who excelled in this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)knowledge would be unfortunate. But the divine power cannot be jealous [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)(nay, according to the proverb, 'bards tell a lie'), nor should any other [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)science be thought more honourable than one of this sort. For the most [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)divine science is also most honourable; and this science alone must be, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)in two ways, most divine. For the science which it would be most meet for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)God to have is a divine science, and so is any science that deals with [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)divine objects; and this science alone has both these qualities; for (1) [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)God is thought to be among the causes of all things and to be a first principle, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and (2) such a science either God alone can have, or God above all others. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)All the sciences, indeed, are more necessary than this, but none is better. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)

"Yet the acquisition of it must in a sense end in something which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)is the opposite of our original inquiries. For all men begin, as we said, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)by wondering that things are as they are, as they do about self-moving [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)marionettes, or about the solstices or the incommensurability of the diagonal [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of a square with the side; for it seems wonderful to all who have not yet [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)seen the reason, that there is a thing which cannot be measured even by [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the smallest unit. But we must end in the contrary and, according to the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)proverb, the better state, as is the case in these instances too when men [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)learn the cause; for there is nothing which would surprise a geometer so [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)much as if the diagonal turned out to be commensurable.

"We have [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)stated, then, what is the nature of the science we are searching for, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)what is the mark which our search and our whole investigation must [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)reach.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)**Part 3**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)"

"Evidently we have to acquire knowledge of the original causes [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)(for we say we know each thing only when we think we recognize its first [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)cause), and causes are spoken of in four senses. In one of these we mean [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the substance, i.e. the essence (for the 'why' is reducible finally to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the definition, and the ultimate 'why' is a cause and principle); in another [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the matter or substratum, in a third the source of the change, and in a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)fourth the cause opposed to this, the purpose and the good (for this is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the end of all generation and change). We have studied these causes sufficiently [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)in our work on nature, but yet let us call to our aid those who have attacked [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the investigation of being and philosophized about reality before us. For [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)obviously they too speak of certain principles and causes; to go over their [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)views, then, will be of profit to the present inquiry, for we shall either [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)find another kind of cause, or be more convinced of the correctness of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)those which we now maintain.

"Of the first philosophers, then, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)most thought the principles which were of the nature of matter were the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)only principles of all things. That of which all things that are consist, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the first from which they come to be, the last into which they are resolved [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)(the substance remaining, but changing in its modifications), this they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)say is the element and this the principle of things, and therefore they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)think nothing is either generated or destroyed, since this sort of entity [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)is always conserved, as we say Socrates neither comes to be absolutely [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)when he comes to be beautiful or musical, nor ceases to be when loses these [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)characteristics, because the substratum, Socrates himself remains. just [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)so they say nothing else comes to be or ceases to be; for there must be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)some entity-either one or more than one-from which all other things come [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to be, it being conserved.

"Yet they do not all agree as to the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)number and the nature of these principles. Thales, the founder of this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)type of philosophy, says the principle is water (for which reason he declared [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)that the earth rests on water), getting the notion perhaps from seeing [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)that the nutriment of all things is moist, and that heat itself is generated [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)from the moist and kept alive by it (and that from which they come to be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)is a principle of all things). He got his notion from this fact, and from [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the fact that the seeds of all things have a moist nature, and that water [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)is the origin of the nature of moist things.

"Some think that even [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the ancients who lived long before the present generation, and first framed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)accounts of the gods, had a similar view of nature; for they made Ocean [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and Tethys the parents of creation, and described the oath of the gods [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)as being by water, to which they give the name of Styx; for what is oldest [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)is most honourable, and the most honourable thing is that by which one [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)swears. It may perhaps be uncertain whether this opinion about nature is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)primitive and ancient, but Thales at any rate is said to have declared [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)himself thus about the first cause. Hippo no one would think fit to include [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)among these thinkers, because of the paltriness of his thought.

"Anaximenes [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and Diogenes make air prior to water, and the most primary of the simple [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)bodies, while Hippasus of Metapontium and Heraclitus of Ephesus say this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of fire, and Empedocles says it of the four elements (adding a fourth-earth-to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)those which have been named); for these, he says, always remain and do [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)not come to be, except that they come to be more or fewer, being aggregated [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)into one and segregated out of one.

"Anaxagoras of Clazomenae, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)who, though older than Empedocles, was later in his philosophical activity, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)says the principles are infinite in number; for he says almost all the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)things that are made of parts like themselves, in the manner of water or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)fire, are generated and destroyed in this way, only by aggregation and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)segregation, and are not in any other sense generated or destroyed, but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)remain eternally.

"From these facts one might think that the only [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)cause is the so-called material cause; but as men thus advanced, the very [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)facts opened the way for them and joined in forcing them to investigate [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the subject. However true it may be that all generation and destruction [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)proceed from some one or (for that matter) from more elements, why does [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)this happen and what is the cause? For at least the substratum itself does [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)not make itself change; e.g. neither the wood nor the bronze causes the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)change of either of them, nor does the wood manufacture a bed and the bronze [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)a statue, but something else is the cause of the change. And to seek this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)is to seek the second cause, as we should say,-that from which comes the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)beginning of the movement. Now those who at the very beginning set themselves [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to this kind of inquiry, and said the substratum was one, were not at all [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)dissatisfied with themselves; but some at least of those who maintain it [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to be one-as though defeated by this search for the second cause-say the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)one and nature as a whole is unchangeable not only in respect of generation [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and destruction (for this is a primitive belief, and all agreed in it), [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)but also of all other change; and this view is peculiar to them. Of those [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)who said the universe was one, then none succeeded in discovering a cause [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of this sort, except perhaps Parmenides, and he only inasmuch as he supposes [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)that there is not only one but also in some sense two causes. But for those [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)who make more elements it is more possible to state the second cause, e.g. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)for those who make hot and cold, or fire and earth, the elements; for they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)treat fire as having a nature which fits it to move things, and water and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)earth and such things they treat in the contrary way.

"When these [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)men and the principles of this kind had had their day, as the latter were [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)found inadequate to generate the nature of things men were again forced [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)by the truth itself, as we said, to inquire into the next kind of cause. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)For it is not likely either that fire or earth or any such element should [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)be the reason why things manifest goodness and, beauty both in their being [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and in their coming to be, or that those thinkers should have supposed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)it was; nor again could it be right to entrust so great a matter to spontaneity [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and chance. When one man said, then, that reason was present-as in animals, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)so throughout nature-as the cause of order and of all arrangement, he seemed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)like a sober man in contrast with the random talk of his predecessors. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)We know that Anaxagoras certainly adopted these views, but Hermotimus of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)Clazomenae is credited with expressing them earlier. Those who thought [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)thus stated that there is a principle of things which is at the same time [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the cause of beauty, and that sort of cause from which things acquire [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)movement.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)**Part 4**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)"

"One might suspect that Hesiod was the first to look for such a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)thing-or some one else who put love or desire among existing things as [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)a principle, as Parmenides, too, does; for he, in constructing the genesis [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of the universe, says:- "

"Love first of all the Gods she planned. "

"And Hesiod says:- "

"First of all things was chaos made, and then

"Broad-breasted [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)earth...

"And love, 'mid all the gods pre-eminent, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)"

which implies that among existing things there must be from the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)first a cause which will move things and bring them together. How these [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)thinkers should be arranged with regard to priority of discovery let us [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)be allowed to decide later; but since the contraries of the various forms [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of good were also perceived to be present in nature-not only order and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the beautiful, but also disorder and the ugly, and bad things in greater [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)number than good, and ignoble things than beautiful-therefore another thinker [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)introduced friendship and strife, each of the two the cause of one of these [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)two sets of qualities. For if we were to follow out the view of Empedocles, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and interpret it according to its meaning and not to its lisping expression, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)we should find that friendship is the cause of good things, and strife [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of bad. Therefore, if we said that Empedocles in a sense both mentions, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and is the first to mention, the bad and the good as principles, we should [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)perhaps be right, since the cause of all goods is the good itself.

"These [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)thinkers, as we say, evidently grasped, and to this extent, two of the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)causes which we distinguished in our work on nature-the matter and the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)source of the movement-vaguely, however, and with no clearness, but as [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)untrained men behave in fights; for they go round their opponents and often [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)strike fine blows, but they do not fight on scientific principles, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)so too these thinkers do not seem to know what they say; for it is evident [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)that, as a rule, they make no use of their causes except to a small extent. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)For Anaxagoras uses reason as a deus ex machina for the making of the world, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and when he is at a loss to tell from what cause something necessarily [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)is, then he drags reason in, but in all other cases ascribes events to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)anything rather than to reason. And Empedocles, though he uses the causes [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to a greater extent than this, neither does so sufficiently nor attains [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)consistency in their use. At least, in many cases he makes love segregate [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)things, and strife aggregate them. For whenever the universe is dissolved [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)into its elements by strife, fire is aggregated into one, and so is each [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of the other elements; but whenever again under the influence of love they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)come together into one, the parts must again be segregated out of each [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)element.

"Empedocles, then, in contrast with his precessors, was [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the first to introduce the dividing of this cause, not positing one source [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of movement, but different and contrary sources. Again, he was the first [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to speak of four material elements; yet he does not use four, but treats [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)them as two only; he treats fire by itself, and its opposite-earth, air, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and water-as one kind of thing. We may learn this by study of his verses. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)

"This philosopher then, as we say, has spoken of the principles [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)in this way, and made them of this number. Leucippus and his associate [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)Democritus say that the full and the empty are the elements, calling the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)one being and the other non-being-the full and solid being being, the empty [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)non-being (whence they say being no more is than non-being, because the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)solid no more is than the empty); and they make these the material causes [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of things. And as those who make the underlying substance one generate [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)all other things by its modifications, supposing the rare and the dense [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to be the sources of the modifications, in the same way these philosophers [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)say the differences in the elements are the causes of all other qualities. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)These differences, they say, are three-shape and order and position. For [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)they say the real is differentiated only by 'rhythm and 'inter-contact' [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and 'turning'; and of these rhythm is shape, inter-contact is order, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)turning is position; for A differs from N in shape, AN from NA in order, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)M from W in position. The question of movement-whence or how it is to belong [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to things-these thinkers, like the others, lazily neglected.

"Regarding [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the two causes, then, as we say, the inquiry seems to have been pushed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)thus far by the early philosophers.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)**Part 5**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)"

"Contemporaneously with these philosophers and before them, the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)so-called Pythagoreans, who were the first to take up mathematics, not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)only advanced this study, but also having been brought up in it they thought [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)its principles were the principles of all things. Since of these principles [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)numbers are by nature the first, and in numbers they seemed to see many [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)resemblances to the things that exist and come into being-more than in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)fire and earth and water (such and such a modification of numbers being [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)justice, another being soul and reason, another being opportunity-and similarly [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)almost all other things being numerically expressible); since, again, they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)saw that the modifications and the ratios of the musical scales were expressible [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)in numbers;-since, then, all other things seemed in their whole nature [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to be modelled on numbers, and numbers seemed to be the first things in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the whole of nature, they supposed the elements of numbers to be the elements [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of all things, and the whole heaven to be a musical scale and a number. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)And all the properties of numbers and scales which they could show to agree [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)with the attributes and parts and the whole arrangement of the heavens, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)they collected and fitted into their scheme; and if there was a gap anywhere, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)they readily made additions so as to make their whole theory coherent. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)E.g. as the number 10 is thought to be perfect and to comprise the whole [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)nature of numbers, they say that the bodies which move through the heavens [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)are ten, but as the visible bodies are only nine, to meet this they invent [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)a tenth--the 'counter-earth'. We have discussed these matters more exactly [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)elsewhere.

"But the object of our review is that we may learn from [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)these philosophers also what they suppose to be the principles and how [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)these fall under the causes we have named. Evidently, then, these thinkers [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)also consider that number is the principle both as matter for things and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)as forming both their modifications and their permanent states, and hold [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)that the elements of number are the even and the odd, and that of these [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the latter is limited, and the former unlimited; and that the One proceeds [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)from both of these (for it is both even and odd), and number from the One; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and that the whole heaven, as has been said, is numbers.

"Other [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)members of this same school say there are ten principles, which they arrange [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)in two columns of cognates-limit and unlimited, odd and even, one and plurality, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)right and left, male and female, resting and moving, straight and curved, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)light and darkness, good and bad, square and oblong. In this way Alcmaeon [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of Croton seems also to have conceived the matter, and either he got this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)view from them or they got it from him; for he expressed himself similarly [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to them. For he says most human affairs go in pairs, meaning not definite [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)contrarieties such as the Pythagoreans speak of, but any chance contrarieties, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)e.g. white and black, sweet and bitter, good and bad, great and small. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)He threw out indefinite suggestions about the other contrarieties, but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the Pythagoreans declared both how many and which their contraricties are. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)

"From both these schools, then, we can learn this much, that the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)contraries are the principles of things; and how many these principles [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)are and which they are, we can learn from one of the two schools. But how [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)these principles can be brought together under the causes we have named [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)has not been clearly and articulately stated by them; they seem, however, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to range the elements under the head of matter; for out of these as immanent [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)parts they say substance is composed and moulded.

"From these facts [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)we may sufficiently perceive the meaning of the ancients who said the elements [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of nature were more than one; but there are some who spoke of the universe [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)as if it were one entity, though they were not all alike either in the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)excellence of their statement or in its conformity to the facts of nature. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)The discussion of them is in no way appropriate to our present investigation [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of causes, for. they do not, like some of the natural philosophers, assume [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)being to be one and yet generate it out of the one as out of matter, but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)they speak in another way; those others add change, since they generate [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the universe, but these thinkers say the universe is unchangeable. Yet [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)this much is germane to the present inquiry: Parmenides seems to fasten [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)on that which is one in definition, Melissus on that which is one in matter, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)for which reason the former says that it is limited, the latter that it [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)is unlimited; while Xenophanes, the first of these partisans of the One [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)(for Parmenides is said to have been his pupil), gave no clear statement, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)nor does he seem to have grasped the nature of either of these causes, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)but with reference to the whole material universe he says the One is God. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)Now these thinkers, as we said, must be neglected for the purposes of the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)present inquiry-two of them entirely, as being a little too naive, viz. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)Xenophanes and Melissus; but Parmenides seems in places to speak with more [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)insight. For, claiming that, besides the existent, nothing non-existent [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)exists, he thinks that of necessity one thing exists, viz. the existent [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and nothing else (on this we have spoken more clearly in our work on nature), [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)but being forced to follow the observed facts, and supposing the existence [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of that which is one in definition, but more than one according to our [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)sensations, he now posits two causes and two principles, calling them hot [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and cold, i.e. fire and earth; and of these he ranges the hot with the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)existent, and the other with the non-existent.

"From what has been [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)said, then, and from the wise men who have now sat in council with us, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)we have got thus much-on the one hand from the earliest philosophers, who [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)regard the first principle as corporeal (for water and fire and such things [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)are bodies), and of whom some suppose that there is one corporeal principle, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)others that there are more than one, but both put these under the head [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of matter; and on the other hand from some who posit both this cause and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)besides this the source of movement, which we have got from some as single [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and from others as twofold.

"Down to the Italian school, then, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and apart from it, philosophers have treated these subjects rather obscurely, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)except that, as we said, they have in fact used two kinds of cause, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)one of these-the source of movement-some treat as one and others as two. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)But the Pythagoreans have said in the same way that there are two principles, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)but added this much, which is peculiar to them, that they thought that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)finitude and infinity were not attributes of certain other things, e.g. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of fire or earth or anything else of this kind, but that infinity itself [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and unity itself were the substance of the things of which they are predicated. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)This is why number was the substance of all things. On this subject, then, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)they expressed themselves thus; and regarding the question of essence they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)began to make statements and definitions, but treated the matter too simply. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)For they both defined superficially and thought that the first subject [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of which a given definition was predicable was the substance of the thing [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)defined, as if one supposed that 'double' and '2' were the same, because [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)2 is the first thing of which 'double' is predicable. But surely to be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)double and to be 2 are not the same; if they are, one thing will be many-a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)consequence which they actually drew. From the earlier philosophers, then, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and from their successors we can learn thus much.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)**Part 6**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)"

"After the systems we have named came the philosophy of Plato, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)which in most respects followed these thinkers, but had pecullarities that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)distinguished it from the philosophy of the Italians. For, having in his [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)youth first become familiar with Cratylus and with the Heraclitean doctrines [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)(that all sensible things are ever in a state of flux and there is no knowledge [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)about them), these views he held even in later years. Socrates, however, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)was busying himself about ethical matters and neglecting the world of nature [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)as a whole but seeking the universal in these ethical matters, and fixed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)thought for the first time on definitions; Plato accepted his teaching, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)but held that the problem applied not to sensible things but to entities [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of another kind-for this reason, that the common definition could not be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)a definition of any sensible thing, as they were always changing. Things [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of this other sort, then, he called Ideas, and sensible things, he said, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)were all named after these, and in virtue of a relation to these; for the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)many existed by participation in the Ideas that have the same name as they. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)Only the name 'participation' was new; for the Pythagoreans say that things [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)exist by 'imitation' of numbers, and Plato says they exist by participation, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)changing the name. But what the participation or the imitation of the Forms [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)could be they left an open question.

"Further, besides sensible [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)things and Forms he says there are the objects of mathematics, which occupy [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)an intermediate position, differing from sensible things in being eternal [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and unchangeable, from Forms in that there are many alike, while the Form [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)itself is in each case unique.

"Since the Forms were the causes [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of all other things, he thought their elements were the elements of all [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)things. As matter, the great and the small were principles; as essential [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)reality, the One; for from the great and the small, by participation in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the One, come the Numbers.

"But he agreed with the Pythagoreans [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)in saying that the One is substance and not a predicate of something else; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and in saying that the Numbers are the causes of the reality of other things [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)he agreed with them; but positing a dyad and constructing the infinite [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)out of great and small, instead of treating the infinite as one, is peculiar [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to him; and so is his view that the Numbers exist apart from sensible things, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)while they say that the things themselves are Numbers, and do not place [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the objects of mathematics between Forms and sensible things. His divergence [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)from the Pythagoreans in making the One and the Numbers separate from things, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and his introduction of the Forms, were due to his inquiries in the region [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of definitions (for the earlier thinkers had no tincture of dialectic), [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and his making the other entity besides the One a dyad was due to the belief [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)that the numbers, except those which were prime, could be neatly produced [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)out of the dyad as out of some plastic material. Yet what happens is the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)contrary; the theory is not a reasonable one. For they make many things [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)out of the matter, and the form generates only once, but what we observe [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)is that one table is made from one matter, while the man who applies the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)form, though he is one, makes many tables. And the relation of the male [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to the female is similar; for the latter is impregnated by one copulation, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)but the male impregnates many females; yet these are analogues of those [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)first principles.

"Plato, then, declared himself thus on the points [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)in question; it is evident from what has been said that he has used only [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)two causes, that of the essence and the material cause (for the Forms are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the causes of the essence of all other things, and the One is the cause [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of the essence of the Forms); and it is evident what the underlying matter [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)is, of which the Forms are predicated in the case of sensible things, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the One in the case of Forms, viz. that this is a dyad, the great and the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)small. Further, he has assigned the cause of good and that of evil to the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)elements, one to each of the two, as we say some of his predecessors sought [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to do, e.g. Empedocles and Anaxagoras.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)**Part 7**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)"

"Our review of those who have spoken about first principles and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)reality and of the way in which they have spoken, has been concise and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)summary; but yet we have learnt this much from them, that of those who [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)speak about 'principle' and 'cause' no one has mentioned any principle [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)except those which have been distinguished in our work on nature, but all [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)evidently have some inkling of them, though only vaguely. For some speak [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of the first principle as matter, whether they suppose one or more first [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)principles, and whether they suppose this to be a body or to be incorporeal; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)e.g. Plato spoke of the great and the small, the Italians of the infinite, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)Empedocles of fire, earth, water, and air, Anaxagoras of the infinity of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)things composed of similar parts. These, then, have all had a notion of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)this kind of cause, and so have all who speak of air or fire or water, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)or something denser than fire and rarer than air; for some have said the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)prime element is of this kind.

"These thinkers grasped this cause [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)only; but certain others have mentioned the source of movement, e.g. those [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)who make friendship and strife, or reason, or love, a principle.

"The [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)essence, i.e. the substantial reality, no one has expressed distinctly. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)It is hinted at chiefly by those who believe in the Forms; for they do [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)not suppose either that the Forms are the matter of sensible things, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the One the matter of the Forms, or that they are the source of movement [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)(for they say these are causes rather of immobility and of being at rest), [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)but they furnish the Forms as the essence of every other thing, and the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)One as the essence of the Forms.

"That for whose sake actions and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)changes and movements take place, they assert to be a cause in a way, but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)not in this way, i.e. not in the way in which it is its nature to be a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)cause. For those who speak of reason or friendship class these causes as [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)goods; they do not speak, however, as if anything that exists either existed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)or came into being for the sake of these, but as if movements started from [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)these. In the same way those who say the One or the existent is the good, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)say that it is the cause of substance, but not that substance either is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)or comes to be for the sake of this. Therefore it turns out that in a sense [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)they both say and do not say the good is a cause; for they do not call [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)it a cause qua good but only incidentally.

"All these thinkers [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)then, as they cannot pitch on another cause, seem to testify that we have [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)determined rightly both how many and of what sort the causes are. Besides [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)this it is plain that when the causes are being looked for, either all [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)four must be sought thus or they must be sought in one of these four ways. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)Let us next discuss the possible difficulties with regard to the way in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)which each of these thinkers has spoken, and with regard to his situation [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)relatively to the first principles.

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)**Part 8**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)"

"Those, then, who say the universe is one and posit one kind of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)thing as matter, and as corporeal matter which has spatial magnitude, evidently [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)go astray in many ways. For they posit the elements of bodies only, not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of incorporeal things, though there are also incorporeal things. And in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)trying to state the causes of generation and destruction, and in giving [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)a physical account of all things, they do away with the cause of movement. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)Further, they err in not positing the substance, i.e. the essence, as the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)cause of anything, and besides this in lightly calling any of the simple [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)bodies except earth the first principle, without inquiring how they are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)produced out of one anothers-I mean fire, water, earth, and air. For some [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)things are produced out of each other by combination, others by separation, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and this makes the greatest difference to their priority and posteriority. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)For (1) in a way the property of being most elementary of all would seem [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)to belong to the first thing from which they are produced by combination, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)and this property would belong to the most fine-grained and subtle of bodies. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)For this reason those who make fire the principle would be most in agreement [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)with this argument. But each of the other thinkers agrees that the element [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of corporeal things is of this sort. At least none of those who named one [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)element claimed that earth was the element, evidently because of the coarseness [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)of its grain. (Of the other three elements each has found some judge on [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)its side; for some maintain that fire, others that water, others that air [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)is the element. Yet why, after all, do they not name earth also, as most [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)men do? For people say all things are earth Hesiod says earth was produced [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)first of corporeal things; so primitive and popular has the opinion been.) [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)According to this argument, then, no one would be right who either says [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the first principle is any of the elements other than fire, or supposes [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)it to be denser than air but rarer than water. But (2) if that which is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)later in generation is prior in nature, and that which is concocted and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)compounded is later in generation, the contrary of what we have been saying [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)must be true,-water must be prior to air, and earth to water.

"So [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)much, then, for those who posit one cause such as we mentioned; but the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)same is true if one supposes more of these, as Empedocles says matter of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)things is four bodies. For he too is confronted by consequences some of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)which are the same as have been mentioned, while others are peculiar to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)him. For we see these bodies produced from one another, which implies that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)the same body does not always remain fire or earth (we have spoken about [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)this in our works on nature); and regarding the cause of movement and the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)question whether we must posit one or two, he must be thought to have spoken [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)neither correctly nor altogether plausibly. And in general, change of quality [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)is necessarily done away with for those who speak thus, for on their view [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)cold will not come from hot nor hot from cold. For if it did there would [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)be something that accepted the contraries themselves, and there would be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)some one entity that became fire and water, which Empedocles denies.

"As [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)regards Anaxagoras, if one were to suppose that he said there were two [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)elements, the supposition would accord thoroughly with an argument which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)Anaxagoras himself did not state articulately, but which he must have accepted [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)if any one had led him on to it. True, to say that in the beginning all [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)things were mixed is absurd both on other grounds and because it follows [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)that they must have existed before in an unmixed form, and because nature [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)does not allow any chance thing to be mixed with any chance thing, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)also because on this view modifications and accidents could be separated [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)from substances (for the same things which are mixed can be separated); [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)yet if one were to follow him up, piecing together what he means, he would [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)perhaps be seen to be somewhat modern in his views. For when nothing was [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)separated out, evidently nothing could be truly asserted of the substance [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)that then existed. I mean, e.g. that it was neither white nor black, nor [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)grey nor any other colour, but of necessity colourless; for if it had been [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)coloured, it would have had one of these colours. And similarly, by this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)same argument, it was flavourless, nor had it any similar attribute; for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)it could not be either of any quality or of any size, nor could it be any [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)definite kind of thing. For if it were, one of the particular forms would [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)have belonged to it, and this is impossible, since all were mixed together; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)for the particular form would necessarily have been already separated out, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)but he all were mixed except reason, and this alone was unmixed and pure. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)From this it follows, then, that he must say the principles are the One [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)(for this is simple and unmixed) and the Other, which is of such a nature [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)as we suppose the indefinite to be before it is defined and partakes of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)some form. Therefore, while expressing himself neither rightly nor clearly, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)he means something like what the later thinkers say and what is now more [](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.1.i.html)clearly seen to be the case.
