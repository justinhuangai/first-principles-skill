**The Art of War**[](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

By Sun Tzu [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

Translated by Lionel Giles

[](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)[](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)**I. Laying Plans**[](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)
**1.** Sun Tzu said: The art of war is of vital importance [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)to the State. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**2.** It is a matter of life and death, a road either to safety [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)or to ruin. Hence it is a subject of inquiry which can on no account be [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)neglected. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**3.** The art of war, then, is governed by five constant factors, [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)to be taken into account in one's deliberations, when seeking to determine [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)the conditions obtaining in the field. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**4.** These are: (1) The Moral Law; (2) Heaven; (3) Earth; [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)(4) The Commander; (5) Method and discipline. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**5,6.** The Moral Law causes the people to be in complete accord [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)with their ruler, so that they will follow him regardless of their lives, [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)undismayed by any danger. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**7.** Heaven signifies night and day, cold and heat, times [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)and seasons. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**8.** Earth comprises distances, great and small; danger and [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)security; open ground and narrow passes; the chances of life and [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)death. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**9.** The Commander stands for the virtues of wisdom, sincerely, [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)benevolence, courage and strictness. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**10.** By method and discipline are to be understood the marshaling [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)of the army in its proper subdivisions, the graduations of rank among the [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)officers, the maintenance of roads by which supplies may reach the army, [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)and the control of military expenditure. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**11.** These five heads should be familiar to every general: [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)he who knows them will be victorious; he who knows them not will [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)fail. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**12.** Therefore, in your deliberations, when seeking to determine [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)the military conditions, let them be made the basis of a comparison, in [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)this wise:-- [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**13.** (1) Which of the two sovereigns is imbued with the Moral [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)law? (2) Which of the two generals has most ability? (3) With whom lie [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)the advantages derived from Heaven and Earth? (4) On which side is discipline [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)most rigorously enforced? (5) Which army is stronger? (6) On which side [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)are officers and men more highly trained? (7) In which army is there the [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)greater constancy both in reward and punishment? [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**14.** By means of these seven considerations I can forecast [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)victory or defeat. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**15.** The general that hearkens to my counsel and acts upon [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)it, will conquer: let such a one be retained in command! The general that [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)hearkens not to my counsel nor acts upon it, will suffer defeat:--let such [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)a one be dismissed! [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**16.** While heading the profit of my counsel, avail yourself [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)also of any helpful circumstances over and beyond the ordinary [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)rules. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**17.** According as circumstances are favorable, one should [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)modify one's plans. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**18.** All warfare is based on deception. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**19.** Hence, when able to attack, we must seem unable; when [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)using our forces, we must seem inactive; when we are near, we must make [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)the enemy believe we are far away; when far away, we must make him believe [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)we are near. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**20.** Hold out baits to entice the enemy. Feign disorder, [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)and crush him. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**21.** If he is secure at all points, be prepared for him. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)If he is in superior strength, evade him. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**22.** If your opponent is of choleric temper, seek to irritate [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)him. Pretend to be weak, that he may grow arrogant. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**23.** If he is taking his ease, give him no rest. If his forces [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)are united, separate them. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**24.** Attack him where he is unprepared, appear where you [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)are not expected. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**25.** These military devices, leading to victory, must not [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)be divulged beforehand. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**26.** Now the general who wins a battle makes many calculations [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)in his temple ere the battle is fought. The general who loses a battle [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)makes but few calculations beforehand. Thus do many calculations lead [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)to victory, and few calculations to defeat: how much more no calculation [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)at all! It is by attention to this point that I can foresee who is likely [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)to win or lose. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**II. Waging War**[](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**1.** Sun Tzu said: In the operations of war, where there [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)are in the field a thousand swift chariots, as many heavy chariots, and [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)a hundred thousand mail-clad soldiers, with provisions enough to carry [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)them a thousand li, the expenditure at home and at the front, including [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)entertainment of guests, small items such as glue and paint, and sums spent [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)on chariots and armor, will reach the total of a thousand ounces of silver [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)per day. Such is the cost of raising an army of 100,000 [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)men. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**2.** When you engage in actual fighting, if victory is long [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)in coming, then men's weapons will grow dull and their ardor will be damped. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html) If you lay siege to a town, you will exhaust your strength. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**3.** Again, if the campaign is protracted, the resources of [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)the State will not be equal to the strain. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**4.** Now, when your weapons are dulled, your ardor damped, [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)your strength exhausted and your treasure spent, other chieftains will [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)spring up to take advantage of your extremity. Then no man, however wise, [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)will be able to avert the consequences that must ensue. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**5.** Thus, though we have heard of stupid haste in war, cleverness [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)has never been seen associated with long delays. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**6.** There is no instance of a country having benefited from [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)prolonged warfare. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**7.** It is only one who is thoroughly acquainted with the [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)evils of war that can thoroughly understand the profitable way of carrying [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)it on. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**8.** The skillful soldier does not raise a second levy, neither [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)are his supply-wagons loaded more than twice. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**9.** Bring war material with you from home, but forage on [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)the enemy. Thus the army will have food enough for its [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)needs. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**10.** Poverty of the State exchequer causes an army to be [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)maintained by contributions from a distance. Contributing to maintain an [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)army at a distance causes the people to be impoverished. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**11.** On the other hand, the proximity of an army causes prices [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)to go up; and high prices cause the people's substance to be drained [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)away. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**12.** When their substance is drained away, the peasantry [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)will be afflicted by heavy exactions. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**13,14.** With this loss of substance and exhaustion of strength, [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)the homes of the people will be stripped bare, and three-tenths of their [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)income will be dissipated; while government expenses for broken chariots, [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)worn-out horses, breast-plates and helmets, bows and arrows, spears and [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)shields, protective mantles, draught-oxen and heavy wagons, will amount [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)to four-tenths of its total revenue. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**15.** Hence a wise general makes a point of foraging on the [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)enemy. One cartload of the enemy's provisions is equivalent to twenty [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)of one's own, and likewise a single picul of his provender is equivalent [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)to twenty from one's own store. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**16.** Now in order to kill the enemy, our men must be roused [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)to anger; that there may be advantage from defeating the enemy, they must [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)have their rewards. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**17.** Therefore in chariot fighting, when ten or more chariots [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)have been taken, those should be rewarded who took the first. Our own flags [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)should be substituted for those of the enemy, and the chariots mingled [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)and used in conjunction with ours. The captured soldiers should be kindly [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)treated and kept. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**18.** This is called, using the conquered foe to augment one's [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)own strength. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**19.** In war, then, let your great object be victory, not [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)lengthy campaigns. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)

**20.** Thus it may be known that the leader of armies is the [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)arbiter of the people's fate, the man on whom it depends whether the nation [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)shall be in peace or in peril. [](http://classics.mit.edu/Tzu/artwar.html)
