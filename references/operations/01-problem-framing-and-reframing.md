# Problem Framing And Reframing

## Why This File Exists

This file compresses the operational work of problem definition, scope control, outcome-first framing, contradiction detection, and reframing into one stronger operational lens.

## Goal

Problem Framing And Reframing gives the skill a stronger, less fragmented operational lens for this class of problem.

## When This Lens Is Usually Right

- the prompt is broad, loaded, or mixes multiple layers together
- the current wording already contains a conclusion disguised as a question
- stakeholders keep debating without stabilizing the object of debate
- the best move is to sharpen the question before proposing action

## Core Questions

- What result is actually being sought?
- What is being smuggled in as an assumption or conclusion?
- What belongs inside the boundary of the question and what does not?
- Which contradiction in the current framing is worth surfacing first?
- What rewritten question would produce a more actionable answer?

## Default Workflow

1. rewrite the problem so the outcome, owner, and horizon are explicit
2. cut scope until one mechanism can be examined honestly
3. name the assumption or conclusion hidden inside the current wording
4. split structural drivers from tactical symptoms and side debates
5. end with the one sharper question that should govern the next move

## Common Patterns

- broad future question -> split by structural forces, household experience, and time horizon
- identity question -> translate into observable commitments and tradeoffs
- strategy request -> separate diagnosis from action before listing options
- cost question -> rewrite from “why is this expensive” to “what is irreducible versus self-inflicted”
- abstract concept -> define the term before making claims about it

## Good Moves

- use scope as a precision tool, not as a disclaimer
- force the question to reveal what would count as solved
- rewrite emotionally loaded phrases into operational language
- treat contradiction as a clue that the system is asking two questions at once
- prefer a smaller honest problem over a grand vague one

## Failure Modes

- answering the inherited wording because it sounds smart
- keeping everything in scope out of fear of missing nuance
- mistaking a slogan for a problem statement
- letting a hidden conclusion survive into the analysis

## Natural Deliverables

- a one-sentence rewritten problem
- a tight in-scope / out-of-scope split
- the contradiction or confusion worth resolving first
- a clearer next question if the original one was too broad

## Example Translation

Question: "中国未来的发展会怎么样？"

A better first-principles move is to split growth, industrial strength, household experience, population structure, and geopolitical pressure before answering. The useful rewrite is: "Over the next 5–15 years, which structural forces are most likely to reshape China’s growth model and ordinary household experience?"

## High-Signal Heuristics

- If the prompt can be read three different ways, the analysis should not start yet.
- A problem that contains its own answer should be rewritten before critique.
- Out-of-scope is part of the answer, not a disclaimer after the answer.
- The cleanest question is often shorter and more measurable than the first version.

## Mini Cases

- A founder asks whether a market is dead; the real question is whether a specific customer pain is still severe enough to pay for.
- A policy question asks whether something is good or bad; the sharper version asks which outcomes, for whom, over what horizon.
- A product debate frames “quality” as a noun; the better framing splits reliability, usability, aesthetic coherence, and support burden.

## Quick Checks

- Does the question define an object, a horizon, and an owner?
- Would two smart people answer two different questions if they used the current wording?
- Is a missing measurement more important than another paragraph of reasoning?

## Useful Prompts

- "What is the cleanest version of this question if we remove the embedded conclusion?"
- "Which sentence in the current framing is doing hidden argumentative work?"
- "What would make this problem small enough to answer without lying?"
- "What should be out of scope if we care about decision quality more than completeness?"
- "Which contradiction in the prompt is actually the main clue?"
- "What result is being sought that the current wording obscures?"
- "If we had to rename this problem in one line, what would we call it?"
- "What is the next question we should answer before we answer this one?"

## Observation Checklist

- State who is deciding, not just what topic is being discussed.
- Make the time horizon visible before comparing outcomes.
- Separate the desired result from the current implementation.
- Look for adjectives that are really untested conclusions.
- Ask whether two different people would answer two different questions here.
- Trim side issues that create breadth without improving the mechanism.
- Surface the first contradiction before proposing a solution set.
- Leave the reader with a tighter problem statement than they started with.

## Red Flags

- The question contains emotionally loaded words but no stable object of analysis.
- People are disagreeing about answers because they are answering different questions.
- The current framing assumes the existing category or institution is the right unit.
- Nuance keeps expanding scope instead of improving precision.
- A recommendation appears before a measurable problem statement exists.
- The conversation rewards eloquence more than discrimination.
- The team cannot say what would count as “solved.”

## Misreadings To Avoid

- Do not turn reframing into endless meta-discussion.
- Do not shrink scope so much that the governing mechanism disappears.
- Do not confuse a neutral tone with a clarified problem.
- Do not preserve bad wording just because stakeholders are attached to it.
- Do not let “both things matter” become an excuse to avoid choosing a boundary.
- Do not leave the user with a better essay but the same fuzzy decision.

## Modern Questions This Helps With

- Why does an AI roadmap feel ambitious but impossible to prioritize?
- What exactly are we debating when we say a product is “premium” or “quality”?
- Why do policy arguments become tribal before the object is even defined?
- How should we restate a geopolitical question so it has a real mechanism?
- Where is a company discussing strategy when it is really discussing identity?
- What is the right boundary for a problem that currently spans product, org, and market?
