# Demand, Jobs, And Market Creation

## Core Figures

Clayton Christensen, Bob Moesta, Peter Drucker

## Why This File Exists

Explain opportunity from the demand side: what progress users want, why current routes fail, and how markets are created or expanded by serving that progress better.

## Source Anchors

- Christensen on jobs to be done and market creation
- Moesta on switching interviews and demand-side progress
- Drucker on contribution and effectiveness

## Distinctions That Matter

- product category versus job-to-be-done
- traffic versus demand quality
- idea novelty versus route-to-progress improvement
- activity volume versus contribution to user result

## Canonical Questions

- What progress is the user trying to make?
- What pushes, pulls, anxieties, and habits shape switching?
- What part of the current route is still painful enough to be rewritten?
- Are we discovering demand or merely describing a category?

## What This Changes In The Skill

- It gives the skill a stronger demand-side lens instead of defaulting to supply or category labels.
- It clarifies when markets are discovered by better serving progress rather than inventing desire.
- It improves opportunity recognition by centering the user’s job and switching conditions.

## Good Moves

- start from struggle and progress rather than from solution identity
- treat switching friction as part of the opportunity map
- define value in terms of changed user state, not marketing adjectives
- look for market creation where a previously excluded user can now make progress cheaply

## Common Misuse

- calling any new technology a demand insight
- using personas where a progress map is needed
- confusing social proof with true demand strength
- optimizing acquisition before understanding the real job

## Example Translation

“What startup opportunities still exist?” is better answered by asking which painful user step can now be shortened, cheapened, or de-risked than by scanning hot categories.

## Import Into The Skill

Use this file when the user is exploring opportunity, growth, user demand, or why a product with obvious intelligence still struggles to be pulled through the market.

## Notes

- Load this when product or startup questions risk being answered in feature language or TAM rhetoric.
- It is especially useful when users appear inconsistent until their progress and tradeoffs are mapped.

## Tension With Neighboring Traditions

- Jobs-to-be-done without route-to-user thinking misses acquisition and adoption reality.
- Category creation stories can become mythology if they stop describing concrete user progress.

## Mini Cases

- A founder searches for “new ideas” and misses an old painful job with a newly cheaper path.
- A product gains attention but not adoption because the switching anxieties were never modeled.
- A market seems small until one user segment can finally access progress at a new price or complexity level.

## Useful Imports

- opportunity scanning
- user progress mapping
- market creation through better routes to old progress

## Diagnostic Prompts

- "What progress is the user trying to make, and what would count as success in their world?"
- "What struggle, friction, or workaround shows the job is already expensive enough to matter?"
- "Which non-consumption pattern hints that market creation is possible?"
- "Why does the current path win today despite obvious pain?"
- "What would make a user switch, and what would keep them from switching?"
- "Which category label is obscuring the actual job to be done?"
- "Where is the opportunity really a cheaper path to an old demand, not a novel desire?"
- "What market appears only after we separate demand from current packaging?"

## Modern Questions This Helps With

- Startup opportunity discovery in AI-enabled markets.
- Reframing “crowded” categories around unmet jobs and switching pain.
- Understanding why users stick with ugly workflows that still get the job done.
- Seeing new distribution as a market-creation tool rather than just growth leverage.
- Separating user progress from product category narratives.
- Finding under-served business processes where demand is hidden in workarounds.
- Explaining why some products are adopted slowly despite obvious feature superiority.
- Spotting where non-consumption is the real opportunity signal.

## What This Tradition Corrects

- trend chasing
- feature-led market reasoning
- category blindness
- TAM theater
- ignoring switching friction
- mistaking awareness for demand

## Translation Patterns

- Turn market size into user progress.
- Turn feature lists into jobs and tradeoffs.
- Turn crowded categories into unmet struggle maps.
- Turn adoption questions into switching analysis.
- Turn buzz into demand primitives.
- Turn startup ideas into rewritten paths to existing jobs.

## Failure Signals

- The answer still sounds like product marketing rather than demand analysis.
- Users are described by demographics instead of progress sought.
- Switching cost remains invisible.
- Opportunity is defined by hype rather than struggle.
- No one can say what existing workaround is being displaced.
- The analysis never reaches why someone would actually change behavior.

## What To Pull Forward Operationally

- One job statement the user would recognize.
- One friction point that makes the opportunity economically real.
- One switching condition that must be satisfied.
- One non-consumption pattern worth testing.
- One rewritten opportunity statement grounded in progress, not buzz.
