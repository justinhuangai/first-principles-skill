# Reality, Experiment, And Engineering Rebuilds

## Core Figures

Galileo Galilei, Richard Feynman, Elon Musk

## Why This File Exists

Ground the skill in observable reality, testing, engineering cost floors, and zero-based rebuild logic instead of inherited industry assumptions.

## Source Anchors

- Galileo on observation over inherited authority
- Feynman on experiment as an anti-self-deception discipline
- Musk-style cost-floor reasoning from materials and physics upward

## Distinctions That Matter

- authority versus observation
- price versus material or engineering floor
- claimed impossibility versus unexamined process cost
- beautiful plan versus tested mechanism

## Canonical Questions

- What does physics or material reality actually forbid here?
- What is the raw or floor-level cost before path dependence is layered on?
- What part of the belief comes from authority rather than direct observation?
- What experiment would expose whether the claim survives contact with reality?

## What This Changes In The Skill

- It shifts attention from inherited prices and authority to physical or operational reality.
- It makes experimentation a way of disciplining claims, not decorating them.
- It supports engineering-style rebuild logic when the current path is mostly historical baggage.

## Good Moves

- strip inherited price and process assumptions out of the starting point
- push from atoms, materials, throughput, and constraints back up to system design
- treat experiment as a humility mechanism, not just an engineering ritual
- rebuild from the floor up when the current system is obviously path dependent

## Common Misuse

- mistaking current market price for irreducible reality
- using “nobody has done it” as causal evidence
- treating experiment as confirmation theater instead of falsification
- assuming engineering is bounded by the present supply chain rather than by deeper constraints

## Example Translation

Rocket cost should first be decomposed into material, manufacturing, reuse, and propellant logic. “Space is expensive” is not an explanation; it is the inherited conclusion that first-principles reasoning is supposed to interrogate.

## Import Into The Skill

Use this file when the problem is about engineering cost floors, canonical physical-world cases, or the difference between tradition and reality.

## Notes

- Load this when the conversation overweights expert consensus and underweights real constraints.
- It is especially useful when someone says something is impossible, too expensive, or already optimized.

## Tension With Neighboring Traditions

- Pure authority skepticism is not enough; the replacement must be observation and test.
- Engineering optimism becomes fantasy if it stops at rhetoric and never reaches physical or cost constraints.

## Mini Cases

- A category is assumed permanently expensive until material and manufacturing floors are decomposed.
- A technical claim sounds impossible until the constraint is shown to be organizational rather than physical.
- A beautiful theory survives in slides but dies in the first real test.

- Turn inherited price into cost stack.

- Authority citations substitute for contact with reality.
- The proposed experiment is decorative and cannot discriminate.
- The analysis accepts industrial history as natural law.
- The output never reaches materials, process, or measured mechanism.
- The redesign move is obvious, but the answer still optimizes the legacy path.
