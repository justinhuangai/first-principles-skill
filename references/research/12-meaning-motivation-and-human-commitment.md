# Meaning, Motivation, And Human Commitment

## Core Figures

Viktor Frankl, Abraham Maslow, Chris Argyris

## Why This File Exists

Give the skill a disciplined way to handle questions of meaning, motivation, and human commitment without collapsing into slogans or pseudo-spiritual certainty.

## Source Anchors

- Frankl on meaning under constraint and suffering
- Maslow on layered needs and motivation structures
- organizational learning traditions that distinguish declared ideals from lived commitment

## Distinctions That Matter

- pleasure versus durable meaning
- stated purpose versus actual commitment pattern
- motivation as rhetoric versus motivation as sustained behavior
- global life answer versus a set of concrete lived commitments

## Canonical Questions

- What remains worth commitment under uncertainty, pain, or time?
- Which needs or constraints shape the person’s motivational structure right now?
- What does the person repeatedly give attention, energy, and sacrifice to in practice?
- How should a large human question be decomposed into lived dimensions rather than answered with a slogan?

## What This Changes In The Skill

- It gives the skill a disciplined way to handle existential and motivational questions without cliché.
- It improves decomposition of large human questions into commitments, needs, attention, and responsibility.
- It keeps “meaning” grounded in lived structure rather than abstract sentiment.

## Good Moves

- split “meaning” into commitment, attention, responsibility, and enduring value
- translate human questions back into observable life structure and repeated action
- avoid pretending one sentence can settle a layered existential problem
- treat motivation as situated, not universally identical across contexts

## Common Misuse

- giving motivational wallpaper instead of decomposition
- confusing short-term reward with stable meaning
- using philosophical language to skip the lived reality layer
- assuming human motivation can be reduced to one metric or one drive

## Example Translation

“What is the meaning of life?” is better handled by decomposing what remains worth commitment, what one is willing to suffer for, and what patterns of responsibility endure, rather than pretending there is one portable answer for all lives.

## Import Into The Skill

Use this file when the user is asking a large human question and the right move is structured clarification rather than a universal slogan.
